Part 2: Equilibrium and Energy

Equilibrium Under the Action of Concurrent Forces

This chapter focuses on systems where all forces acting on an object pass through a single point (concurrent forces). The key concept is that an object is in equilibrium when the vector sum of all forces acting on it is zero. This leads to two conditions for equilibrium:

  1. The First Condition for Equilibrium: ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0. (The sum of the x-components and y-components of all forces must be zero).
  2. This condition is sufficient for equilibrium if the forces are concurrent. For non-concurrent forces, we need the second condition as well.

The chapter provides a systematic problem-solving method:

  1. Isolate the object for discussion.
  2. Show the forces acting on the isolated object in a diagram (free-body diagram).
  3. Find the rectangular components of each force.
  4. Write the first condition for equilibrium in equation form.
  5. Solve for the required quantities.

Specific types of forces are discussed:

  • Weight of an object (Fw): The force of gravity pulling downward.
  • Tensile force (FT): The force in a string, cable, or tendon.
  • Friction force (Ff): A force opposing the sliding of one surface across another. The chapter introduces both static and kinetic friction, and defines the coefficient of friction (μ).
  • Normal force (FN): The component of the supporting force that is perpendicular to the surface.

The concept of dimensional analysis is also introduced as a way to check the validity of equations and ensure consistency of units.

Equilibrium of a Rigid Body Under Coplanar Forces

This chapter extends the equilibrium concepts to rigid bodies subjected to forces that lie in a single plane (coplanar forces). A new concept, torque (or moment), is introduced. Torque is the measure of the effectiveness of a force in producing rotation around an axis. It's defined as:

Torque = rF sin θ

where:

  • r is the radial distance from the axis to the point of application of the force.
  • F is the magnitude of the force.
  • θ is the angle between the line of action of F and the line from the axis to the point where F is applied.

Two conditions are necessary for equilibrium of a rigid body under coplanar forces:

  1. The First (or Force) Condition: The vector sum of all forces acting on the body must be zero: ΣF = 0
  2. The Second (or Torque) Condition: The sum of all torques acting on the body about any axis must be zero: Στ = 0

The chapter introduces the center of gravity as the point at which the entire weight of the object may be considered concentrated. The concept of choosing an axis for calculating torque is also discussed to simplify calculations.

Work, Energy, and Power

This chapter delves into the concepts of work, energy, and power.

  • Work done by a force is defined as the product of the force and the parallel distance over which it acts. The formula is: W = F||s = Fs cos θ, where θ is the angle between the force and the displacement vector.
  • Energy is the capacity of a system to do work. The chapter focuses on:
    • Kinetic energy (KE): The energy possessed by an object because it is in motion. KE = (1/2)mv2
    • Potential energy (PE): The energy possessed by an object because of its position or configuration. The chapter discusses gravitational potential energy (PE = mgh) and elastic potential energy (PE = (1/2)kx2 for a spring).
  • Work-energy theorem: states that the net work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.
  • Conservation of energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. This principle is a cornerstone of physics.
  • Power is the time rate of doing work. Average power is given by: P = W/t = Fv cos θ

The chapter uses numerous solved problems illustrating the application of these concepts, including calculations involving inclined planes and various types of forces.

Simple Machines

This chapter introduces simple machines and their applications. Simple machines are devices that can change the magnitude, direction, or method of application of a force.

  • Principle of Work: For a simple machine, the work done by the input force equals the work done on the load.
  • Mechanical Advantage (MA): The ratio of the force exerted by the machine on a load to the force used to operate the machine. The chapter distinguishes between actual mechanical advantage (AMA) and ideal mechanical advantage (IMA).
  • Efficiency: The ratio of output work to input work. It accounts for energy losses due to friction.

Solved problems cover levers, pulleys, wheel and axle, inclined planes, and jacks, showing how to calculate MA, AMA, IMA, and efficiency for various scenarios.
