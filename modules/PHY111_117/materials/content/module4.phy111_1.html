Introduction

This section lays the groundwork for the entire book by introducing fundamental concepts in mechanics. We'll start with essential mathematical tools and then move on to core principles of motion and forces.

1. Introduction to Vectors

Scalar and Vector Quantities

Physics utilizes both scalar and vector quantities. A scalar quantity, like temperature or mass, is fully described by its magnitude. A vector quantity, such as displacement or force, requires both magnitude and direction. Vectors are graphically represented by arrows, where the length corresponds to the magnitude and the arrowhead points in the direction.

Vector Addition

Vectors are added using either the graphical method (placing vectors head-to-tail and drawing the resultant) or the parallelogram method (forming a parallelogram with the vectors as adjacent sides; the diagonal represents the resultant). The component method involves resolving vectors into their x and y (and possibly z) components, adding the components separately, and then finding the magnitude and direction of the resultant vector.

Example: Two displacement vectors, 20 m at 40° and 40 m at 137°, can be added graphically or using the component method to find the resultant displacement.

Vector Subtraction

Subtracting vector B from vector A is equivalent to adding the negative of B (same magnitude, opposite direction) to A.

Unit Vectors

Unit vectors (î, ĵ, k̂) have a magnitude of one and point along the x, y, and z axes respectively. They provide a convenient way to express vectors in component form.

2. Uniformly Accelerated Motion

Speed and Velocity

Speed is a scalar quantity (distance/time). Velocity is a vector quantity (displacement/time), possessing both magnitude and direction. Instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a specific instant, while average velocity is the overall displacement divided by the total time.

Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity (vector quantity). It's calculated as the change in velocity divided by the time interval. Uniformly accelerated motion along a straight line is a key concept, described by five equations relating displacement, initial velocity, final velocity, acceleration, and time.

Acceleration Due to Gravity

The acceleration due to gravity (g) is approximately 9.81 m/s² directed vertically downward. This constant acceleration plays a crucial role in projectile motion problems.

Velocity Components

For projectile motion (motion under gravity), velocities are resolved into horizontal and vertical components. The equations of uniformly accelerated motion are applied separately to each component to solve for various aspects of the trajectory.

Projectile Motion

Projectile motion combines horizontal (constant velocity) and vertical (uniformly accelerated) motion. Solved problems in this section demonstrate how to determine range, maximum height, time of flight, and other parameters of projectile trajectories.

Solved Problems

Numerous solved problems cover topics such as calculating average speed, determining instantaneous velocity from distance-time graphs, analyzing projectile motion in detail, and investigating various one-dimensional motion scenarios.

3. Newton's Laws

Mass and Force

Mass is a measure of an object's inertia (resistance to change in motion). The standard kilogram is the fundamental unit of mass. Force is the agent of change in motion, a vector quantity possessing magnitude and direction. Newton's second law (F=ma) quantifies this relationship: force equals mass times acceleration.

Newton's Three Laws

  1. Newton's First Law (Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.
  2. Newton's Second Law: The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass (F=ma).
  3. Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction): For every action, there's an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always occur in pairs.

Weight

Weight is the gravitational force acting on an object. On Earth, it's approximately mg, where m is the mass and g is the acceleration due to gravity. Weight is a force, thus it's a vector.

Other Forces

  • Tension: Force in a rope or string.
  • Friction: Force opposing motion between surfaces.
  • Normal Force: Force perpendicular to a supporting surface.

Coefficients of static and kinetic friction are introduced and used in problem-solving.

Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis, checking the consistency of units in equations, is explained, serving as a valuable tool for error checking and problem-solving.
