Part 3: Fluid Dynamics and Thermodynamics

Fluids at Rest

Average Pressure

The average pressure on a surface of area A is found as force divided by area, where it is stipulated that the force must be perpendicular (normal) to the area:

Average pressure = force acting normal to an area / area over which the force is distributed

Recall that the SI unit for pressure is the pascal (Pa). 1 Pa = 1 N/m2.

Standard Atmospheric Pressure

1 atmosphere (atm) ≈ 1.01 x 105 Pa; this is equivalent to 14.7 lb/in2.

Other units used for pressure:

  • 1 torr = 1 mm of mercury (mm Hg) ≈ 133.3 Pa
  • 1 bar = 105 Pa

Hydrostatic Pressure

Hydrostatic pressure due to a column of fluid of height h and mass density ρ is:

P = ρgh

Pascal's Principle

When the pressure on any part of a confined fluid (liquid or gas) is changed, the pressure on every other part of the fluid is also changed by the same amount.

Archimedes' Principle

A body wholly or partly immersed in a fluid is buoyed up by a force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced. The buoyant force can be considered to act vertically upward through the center of gravity of the displaced fluid.

Fluids in Motion

Fluid Flow or Discharge

When a fluid (like oil) flows through a pipe, the fluid flow or discharge rate is:

J = Av

where A is the cross-sectional area of the pipe.

Equation of Continuity

Suppose that an incompressible (constant-density) fluid fills a pipe and flows through it. Suppose further that the cross-sectional areas are A1 at one point and A2 at another. Since the fluid flow must equal the flow through A2, one has:

A1v1 = A2v2 (constant)

Shear Rate

The shear rate is the rate at which the shear strain within the fluid is changing. Because shear has no units, the SI unit for shear rate is s-1.

Viscosity

Viscosity (η) is a measure of how large a shear stress is required to produce a unit shear rate. Its SI unit is that of stress per unit shear rate, or Pa · s.

Poiseuille's Law

The fluid flow through a cylindrical pipe of length L and cross-sectional radius r is given by:

J = (πr4/8ηL)(P1 - P2)

Work Done by a Piston

The work done by a piston in forcing a volume V of fluid into a cylinder against an opposing pressure P is given by PV.

Work Done by a Moving Surface

The work done on a surface of area A as the surface moves through a distance Δx normal to the surface (thereby displacing a volume A Δx) is:

Work = P(A Δx) = PΔV

Bernoulli's Equation

Let fluid flow at a height h1 and let v1 and P1 be the fluid speed and pressure at that point. Similarly define h2, v2, and P2 for point 2. Then, provided the fluid is incompressible and has negligible viscosity:

P1 + ρgh1 + ½ρv12 = P2 + ρgh2 + ½ρv22

Torricelli's Theorem

Suppose that a tank contains liquid and is open to the atmosphere at its top. If an orifice (opening) exists in the tank at a distance h below the top of the liquid, then the speed of flow from the orifice to the free liquid below the top is:

v ≈ √(2gh)

Reynolds Number

The Reynolds number is a dimensionless number that applies to a fluid of viscosity η and density ρ flowing with speed v through a pipe or past an obstacle with diameter D:

R = ρvD/η

Thermodynamics

Heat

Heat (ΔQ) is the thermal energy that flows from one body or system to another, which is in thermal equilibrium with its surroundings. Heat always flows from hot to cold.

Internal Energy

The internal energy (ΔU) of a system is the total energy content of the system. It is the sum of all forms of energy possessed by the atoms and molecules of the system.

Work Done by a System

When a system expands doing work on the surroundings at a constant pressure P, the work done is given by:

ΔW = PΔV

First Law of Thermodynamics

The first law of thermodynamics is a statement of the law of conservation of energy. It states that if an amount of heat ΔQ flows into or out of a system, then this energy must appear as changed internal energy ΔU of the system and/or as work done by the system on its surroundings:

ΔQ = ΔU + ΔW

Isobaric Process

An isobaric process is a process carried out at constant pressure.

Isovolumetric Process

An isovolumetric process is a process carried out at constant volume.

Isothermal Process

An isothermal process is a process carried out at constant temperature. However, this is true for many systems only approximately.

Adiabatic Process

An adiabatic process is one in which no heat is transferred to or from the system. For such a process, ΔQ = 0.

Specific Heats of Gases

When a gas is heated at constant volume, the heat supplied increases only the internal energy of the molecules; the energy of the molecules does not do mechanical work in expanding the gas against the opposing pressure.

Specific Heat Ratio

The specific heat ratio indicates that for monatomic gases such as He, Ne, A, etc., this ratio is greater than unity.

Work Related to Area

The work done by a fluid in an expansion is equal to the area beneath the expansion curve on a p-V diagram.

Efficiency of a Heat Engine

The Carnot cycle is the most efficient possible cycle for a heat engine.
